Message from the Founder

A Word Before the Numbers

When Poverty 360 published its last annual report in 2021, we had just completed our most ambitious year as an organisation — a Climate Year that planted 10,000 seedlings, reached 3,000 households with clean cookstoves, and demonstrated that a small organisation with a clear evidence base can punch well above its weight.

Then came a period of deliberate transition. Between 2022 and 2024, I made a considered decision to expand the organisation's methodological foundations — deepening my own expertise in government digital services, Medicaid analytics, and decision-support systems through direct work with USAID, UNDP, and UKAID programmes worth over $200 million. That knowledge is now embedded in how Poverty 360 designs and evaluates its programmes.

During this period, we did not stop working. In 2023, we launched BIRTHSAFE Niger — our most medically critical programme to date — bringing life-saving misoprostol access to 1,400 women in the remote Tillabéri and Diffa regions through 200 trained traditional birth attendants. In a country where postpartum haemorrhage kills more mothers than any other cause, this work is measured not in metrics but in lives.

In 2025, we turned to Ghana's Western Region, where illegal mining has rendered the Tano and Ankobra Rivers — once lifelines for entire communities — toxic with mercury and cyanide. Our CLEARWATER Ghana programme distributed 400 water purification systems to 2,400 households in Alubo, Samreboi, and surrounding mining-affected communities, ensuring that families who cannot wait for the rivers to heal do not drink water that will harm them.

This report tells those stories honestly — including the transition period, including the gap, and including our forward commitments. We believe that transparency is itself a programme outcome.

Sherriff Abdul-Hamid Founder & Executive Director, Poverty 360

BIRTHSAFE Niger
Maternal Mortality Prevention — Tillabéri & Diffa

When the Nearest Clinic Is a Day Away

In Niger's most remote communities, women die not from lack of knowledge — but from lack of access. BIRTHSAFE puts life-saving medicine in the hands of the people already trusted to deliver babies.

BIRTHSAFE Niger · Tillabéri & Diffa Regions · 2023–2024

"The potential for saving maternal lives increases substantially when community-based birth attendants are trained to use misoprostol and connected to reliable drug supply chains."
World Health Organization · Misoprostol Guidelines for PPH Prevention, 2012

The BIRTHSAFE Model

01
Identify & Select

200 traditional birth attendants (TBAs) identified in Tillabéri and Diffa — the women already attending births in communities with no skilled facility access.

02
Train

TBAs trained in the appropriate use of misoprostol — dosage, timing of administration, recognition of PPH signs, and when to trigger a facility referral.

03
Link to Supply

Formal supply linkages created between TBAs and district health facilities — giving them authorised, replenishable access to misoprostol stock without requiring women to travel.

04
Monitor & Refer

Referral pathways established for TBAs to escalate complications to the nearest health facility, supported by community health worker liaison roles.

1,400 Women reached with PPH prevention coverage

Why Tillabéri and Diffa?

Tillabéri region in western Niger has historically faced the double burden of low skilled birth attendance and long distances to health infrastructure — with communities spread across semi-arid terrain where a health centre may serve populations 40–80 kilometres away.

Diffa, in southeastern Niger near the Lake Chad basin, faces the additional challenge of instability and displacement — meaning that even where health facilities exist, women may be unable to safely reach them at the moment of delivery. In both regions, the traditional birth attendant is not a last resort — she is the primary care provider at the moment of highest risk.

The Evidence Behind the Model

Community-based misoprostol distribution for PPH prevention has been rigorously evaluated across Pakistan, India, Nepal, Afghanistan, Indonesia, and throughout sub-Saharan Africa. Coverage rates are highest when traditional birth attendants and community health workers distribute the medication — outperforming facility-based distribution in remote settings.

Niger's own national PPH initiative demonstrated that near-universal uterotonic coverage is achievable at scale when supply chains and training are aligned. BIRTHSAFE applies those national lessons at the community level, in two of the country's most underserved regions.

The Galamsey Water Crisis — Western Region, Ghana

Illegal small-scale gold mining — locally known as galamsey — has contaminated over 60% of Ghana's water bodies, according to the Ghana Water Company. Mercury, cyanide, arsenic, and cadmium from unregulated mining operations flow directly into the Tano River and the Ankobra River — the primary water sources for communities in Alubo, Samreboi, and surrounding Western Region towns. Residents of these communities face waterborne diseases, heavy metal poisoning, kidney damage, and elevated risks of cancer — health consequences that accumulate over years of exposure to contaminated water. Children and pregnant women bear the highest burden. The crisis is not new. The response, for most of these communities, has not come.

Alubo and Samreboi: The Communities We Serve

Alubo sits on the Ghana–Côte d'Ivoire border in the Jomoro District — a trading town of considerable economic activity but minimal infrastructure investment. The community draws water from the Tano River, which has been progressively contaminated by upstream mining operations. Alubo's residents have no alternative municipal water supply.

Samreboi lies in the Amenfi West area, directly on the Tano River. A town of approximately 29,000 people and 20 surrounding villages — historically dependent on the river and on the Samartex timber industry — it has seen its primary water source rendered unsafe by mining discharge from operations stretching from Sefwi Wiawso downstream. Research confirms that mercury concentrations in the Tano basin, while variable, have exceeded WHO safe thresholds at specific monitoring points.

The Ankobra River, which flows 190 kilometres from its source northeast of Wiawso to the Gulf of Guinea, is similarly polluted — with entire communities along its course relying on water that the Ghana Water Company has declared unfit for human consumption.

Health Evidence

Research by the Ghana Health Service documents that respiratory disease cases in galamsey-affected regions reached 560 per 100,000 people in 2024 — more than double the rate in unaffected areas. Heavy metal poisoning via fish and water consumption is documented in the Tano and Ankobra basins. About 70% of households in mining-affected communities rely on surface water as their primary source.

Why Water Purifiers

Household water purification systems represent the most immediate, community-deployable intervention available when river decontamination — a process taking years and requiring government action — cannot be waited for. Point-of-use purification removes biological contaminants and reduces exposure to heavy metals, providing protection while the structural pollution crisis is addressed at the policy level.

The CLEARWATER Delivery Model

01
Community Mapping

Identified highest-need households in Alubo, Samreboi, and surrounding Tano and Ankobra basin communities based on proximity to contamination sources and absence of alternative water access.

02
Distribution

400 water purification systems distributed through community focal points — leveraging existing community health structures to ensure equitable reach across households.

03
Usage Training

Community health volunteers trained on correct purifier use, maintenance, and filter replacement — ensuring sustained effectiveness beyond distribution day.

04
Advocacy

Programme findings documented and shared with Western Region environmental health authorities — contributing to the evidence base for policy action on galamsey water contamination.

Looking Ahead — CLEARWATER Expansion

The 2025 CLEARWATER programme covered an initial cohort of 2,400 households. Research indicates that communities along the full extent of the Tano River basin — including Elubo, Essiama, Aiyinase, and Tano Dumasi — face the same contamination burden. Poverty 360 is developing a proposal to extend CLEARWATER coverage to an additional 5,000 households in 2026, contingent on funding from bilateral donors and Ghana-based community development funds. Strategic Direction

Looking Forward: 2026 and Beyond

Poverty 360 enters 2026 with three parallel tracks — none of which is new. All three are expressions of the same foundational question: how do scarce resources reach the people most likely to be excluded from formal systems?

Track 1 · West Africa

CLEARWATER Expansion

Extend the CLEARWATER programme to 5,000 additional households across the Tano and Ankobra River basins. Engage Western Region authorities on the structural advocacy case for galamsey remediation. Continue BIRTHSAFE Niger with a second cohort of 200 traditional birth attendants targeting Agadez and Tahoua regions.

Track 2 · United States

Government Digital Services

Deepen Poverty 360's US presence through advisory work with Medicaid managed care organisations, Federally Qualified Health Centres, and USAID implementing partners. The analytical tools developed during 2022–2024 are the foundation for a fee-for-service technical assistance model that funds the Africa-facing work.

Track 3 · Future Platform

Social Protection Systems

Poverty 360 is developing a modular social protection management platform — a lightweight, open-architecture system purpose-built for low-capacity government environments in West Africa and Southeast Asia. Pilot scoping in 2026. First deployment target: Ghana's Ministry of Social Welfare.
